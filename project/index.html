Project Steps & Required Deliverables

1. Dataset Selection

What to do:

  • Choose a dataset that interests you and has real-world relevance
  • Look for something substantial but not overwhelming
  • Suggested sources: Kaggle, UCI ML Repository, government open data portals

Dataset Requirements:

  • Minimum 1,000 observations
  • At least 8 features
  • Clear prediction target (classification or regression)
  • Real-world data (not pre-cleaned "toy" datasets)

Deliverable: Dataset source, link, and brief rationale (1-2 paragraphs) in your report

2. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

What to do:

  • Analyze data structure, types, distributions, and relationships
  • Create meaningful visualizations (histograms, scatter plots, correlation heatmaps, box plots, etc.)
  • Identify patterns, anomalies, and data quality issues
  • Document insights that inform your modeling decisions

Deliverable in Report: Summary of EDA findings with at least 6 meaningful plots

Deliverable in Colab: Well-documented EDA notebook with clear markdown explanations

3. Data Cleaning and Preprocessing

What to do:

  • Handle missing values (document your strategy)
  • Address outliers and anomalies
  • Encode categorical variables
  • Scale/normalize numerical features
  • Engineer meaningful new features if appropriate
  • Create train/test split (or train/validation/test)

Deliverable: Clear documentation of preprocessing steps in both code and report

4. Model Development and Evaluation

What to do:

  • Implement and compare at least 2 different model types (e.g., logistic regression, decision trees, random forest, gradient boosting, neural networks)
  • Use appropriate evaluation metrics for your problem type
  • Perform hyperparameter tuning on your best models
  • Include cross-validation where appropriate
  • Compare model performance using metrics, confusion matrices, ROC curves, etc.

Deliverable: Complete model comparison with evaluation metrics, visualizations, and interpretation

üí° About the Model Requirement: The requirement is a minimum of 2 models to ensure you can compare approaches. Many students find it natural to try 3-4 models as they explore different techniques. Quality analysis of 2 well-chosen models is better than superficial implementation of many models!

5. Interpretability and Business Insights

What to do:

  • Interpret model results in business context
  • Identify most important features and explain their impact
  • Generate actionable recommendations based on your findings
  • Consider model limitations and appropriate use cases

Deliverable: Business-focused interpretation section in report with clear recommendations

6. Ethics and Responsible AI Reflection

What to consider:

  • Potential biases in data or model predictions
  • Fairness considerations across different groups
  • Privacy and data security implications
  • Responsible deployment considerations
  • Transparency and explainability requirements

Deliverable: 1-2 page ethics reflection in your report

Final Report Requirements

Format: Professional business report (PDF or Markdown), 10-12 pages

Audience: Write for a business-savvy reader who may not know machine learning details but wants to understand your approach and findings

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I use a dataset from a previous course?

A: Yes, if you're doing a substantially different analysis with different models and questions. Please check with me first to ensure there's enough new content.

Q: Is 4 weeks enough time to complete this project?

A: Absolutely! This timeline is designed based on successful past projects. With 10-15 hours per week of focused work, you'll have plenty of time for quality analysis. The weekly checkpoints help you pace yourself effectively.

Q: How should I approach Thanksgiving week?

A: Many students find it helpful to make good progress in Week 1 so they can enjoy Thanksgiving break or work at a lighter pace. Others prefer to work steadily through all four weeks. Choose the approach that fits your schedule and energy best!

Q: Can I change my dataset after starting?

A: Yes, especially early in the project. If you realize your dataset isn't working well, it's better to switch early (ideally within the first week or two) than to struggle with a problematic dataset. Just let me know if you need to make a change.

Q: What if my models perform poorly?

A: That's completely okay! Real-world data doesn't always produce perfect predictions. What matters is demonstrating proper methodology, showing what you tried, and thoughtfully explaining the challenges. Honest analysis of limitations shows professional maturity.

Q: Should I focus more on the GitHub repository or the report?

A: Both are important! The report demonstrates your understanding and insights; GitHub demonstrates your technical skills and creates a portfolio piece. They complement each other.

Q: What if my GitHub repository gets private accidentally?

A: No problem! Just make sure it's public before the deadline. Double-check the visibility settings when you submit.

Q: How technical should my report be?

A: Write for a business-savvy reader who may not know machine learning details. Include enough technical information to demonstrate your work, but focus on clear explanations and business implications.

Q: Do I really need at least 2 models?

A: Yes, comparing different approaches is essential for understanding what works best for your problem. Most students naturally explore 2-3 models as they learn which techniques suit their data. Quality comparison of 2 models is better than superficial implementation of many!

Q: Can I get feedback before the final deadline?

A: Absolutely! Office hours are perfect for getting feedback on your dataset selection, approach, or preliminary results. The earlier you check in, the more time you have to incorporate suggestions.
